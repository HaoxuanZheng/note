# Digital SAT Master Guide (2025–2026)

You’re aiming high, so this guide is built for that—1500+ mindset, not survival mode. Use it as a playbook, not a textbook: read, apply, and constantly loop back through mistakes.

## 1. Digital SAT overview 2025–2026

### 1.1 Structure, timing, and adaptivity

| Section | Modules | Questions | Time | Adaptive? | Score |
|--------|---------|-----------|------|-----------|-------|
| Reading & Writing | 2 (RW1, RW2) | 27 + 27 = 54 | 32 + 32 = 64 min | Yes | 200–800 |
| Math | 2 (M1, M2) | 22 + 22 = 44 | 35 + 35 = 70 min | Yes | 200–800 |
| Total | 4 modules | 98 | 2h 14m (+10m break) | Yes | 400–1600 |

- **Module 1 (each section):** Same difficulty mix for everyone (easy/medium/hard).  [The Test Advantage](https://thetestadvantage.com/blog/digital-sat-format-2026-sections-timing-scoring-guide)  [test-ninjas.com](https://test-ninjas.com/digital-sat-format)  
- **Module 2 (each section):** Difficulty depends on Module 1 performance:
  - **Hard module:** Higher difficulty ceiling, allows 700–800 range.
  - **Easier module:** Lower difficulty ceiling; even perfect performance caps around mid‑600s to ~650–680 depending on test.  [The Test Advantage](https://thetestadvantage.com/blog/digital-sat-format-2026-sections-timing-scoring-guide)  

**Key implication:**  
**Module 1 is leverage.** Strong performance there “unlocks” the high‑scoring module. Your job: high accuracy without burning time.

### 1.2 Scoring basics

- **Raw score:** Number of questions correct (no penalty for wrong answers).  [test-ninjas.com](https://test-ninjas.com/digital-sat-format)  
- **Scaled section scores:** Each section (RW, Math) converted to **200–800**; combined for **400–1600**.
- **Adaptive effect:** Two students with the same number of wrong answers can get different scores if they saw different difficulty modules.

You won’t see the exact conversion table (it varies by form), so focus on:

- **RW:** Miss ≤3–4 questions total for 750+, ≤1–2 for 780–800.  
- **Math:** Miss ≤1–2 for 750+, often 0–1 for 790–800.

(Those are typical patterns from released tests and tutor data, not official guarantees.)

## 2. Reading & Writing – complete content breakdown

Official domains and approximate distributions (from College Board framework + recent analyses):  [College Board](https://satsuite.collegeboard.org/media/pdf/assessment-framework-for-digital-sat-suite.pdf)  [The Test Advantage](https://thetestadvantage.com/blog/digital-sat-format-2026-sections-timing-scoring-guide)  

| Domain | Approx. Share | Focus |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| Information & Ideas | ~25–30% | Main ideas, evidence, inferences, data |
| Craft & Structure | ~25–30% | Words in context, structure, purpose, cross‑text |
| Expression of Ideas | ~20% | Clarity, concision, transitions, synthesis |
| Standard English Conventions | ~20–25% | Grammar, usage, punctuation |

Each question is tied to a **very short passage** (1–3 sentences, sometimes a small graph). One question per passage.

Below: for each skill—format, strategy, 2 examples, traps.

---

### 2.1 Category A – Information and Ideas

#### A1. Central ideas and details

**Format:**  
Short passage (often 2–4 sentences). Question: *“Which choice best states the main idea of the text?”* or *“Which choice best describes what the text is primarily about?”*

**Step‑by‑step strategy:**

1. **Read for purpose, not details.** Ask: “What is the author trying to do overall?”  
2. **Summarize in 5–7 words** in your head.  
3. **Eliminate:**
   - Too narrow (only one example).
   - Too broad (goes beyond passage).
   - Distorted (adds opinions not present).  
4. Choose the answer that matches your mini‑summary **without adding new claims**.

**Example 1**

> *Many city planners are rethinking parking minimums—rules that require new buildings to include a certain number of parking spaces. They argue that these rules encourage car use, increase housing costs, and waste valuable land that could be used for parks or homes instead.*

**Question:** Which choice best states the main idea of the text?

A. City planners are eliminating all parking spaces in new buildings.  
B. Parking minimums can have negative consequences for cities.  
C. New buildings should always include more parking than required.  
D. Parks and homes are more important than cars.

**Solution:**

- The passage: planners are **rethinking parking minimums** because they **encourage car use, raise costs, waste land**.  
- **A:** Too extreme (“eliminating all”).  
- **C:** Opposite (“more parking”).  
- **D:** Value judgment not stated.  
- **B** matches: negative consequences of parking minimums → **Correct: B**.

**Common traps:**

- **Extreme language:** “always,” “never,” “all,” “only.”  
- **Opinion injection:** Answer sounds nice but isn’t actually stated.

**Example 2**

> *In a recent study, participants who took short walking breaks during a long task reported feeling more focused and less fatigued than those who worked without breaks. The researchers suggest that even brief movement can restore attention.*

Question: The text primarily supports which claim?

A. Long tasks are impossible without frequent breaks.  
B. Walking breaks are the only way to restore attention.  
C. Short walking breaks may help people stay focused.  
D. People should avoid working on long tasks.

Correct: **C** (hedged, matches evidence; others are extreme or unrelated).

---

#### A2. Command of evidence (textual)

**Format:**  
Question asks which choice **best supports** a claim or **provides the strongest evidence** for a statement.

**Strategy:**

1. **Identify the claim** in the question.  
2. **Scan the passage** for the sentence that directly backs that claim (data, explanation, example).  
3. Choose the answer that **most directly and specifically** supports it.

**Example 1**

> *Claim: Online language‑learning platforms can make studying more flexible for adults with busy schedules.*

Passage:

> *Unlike traditional classes, online language‑learning platforms allow users to choose when and where they study. Many adults log in during lunch breaks or late evenings, times when in‑person classes are rarely available.*

Question: Which sentence best supports the claim?

A. Many adults prefer learning languages in groups.  
B. Online platforms let users study at times that fit their schedules.  
C. Some people find online learning less engaging than in‑person classes.  
D. Language learning can be challenging for adults.

Correct: **B** (directly supports flexibility).

**Traps:**

- Vague “related” sentences that mention the topic but don’t actually prove the claim.
- Interesting but **off‑focus** details.

---

#### A3. Command of evidence (quantitative)

**Format:**  
Short text + graph/table. Question: *“Which choice best uses the data to support the claim?”* or *“According to the graph, which statement is accurate?”*

**Strategy:**

1. **Read the axes/labels carefully.**  
2. **Translate the question**: what relationship or number do we need?  
3. **Check each answer directly against the graph**—no estimation beyond what’s shown.  
4. Eliminate answers that:
   - Reverse the relationship.
   - Use wrong units.
   - Exaggerate beyond the data.

**Example**

Text:  

> *The author claims that more students are choosing community colleges over four‑year universities.*

Graph:  
- 2018–2022: Community college enrollment rises from 1.0M to 1.4M.  
- University enrollment falls from 2.0M to 1.8M.

Question: Which statement best uses the graph to support the claim?

A. Community college enrollment stayed the same from 2018 to 2022.  
B. University enrollment increased while community college enrollment decreased.  
C. Community college enrollment increased while university enrollment decreased between 2018 and 2022.  
D. Community colleges now enroll more students than universities.

Correct: **C** (matches data; D goes beyond—universities still larger).

---

#### A4. Inferences

**Format:**  
Question: *“Which choice is the best inference?”* or *“The text most strongly suggests that…”*

**Strategy:**

1. **Base everything on explicit text.**  
2. Ask: “If this is true, what else must be true or is very likely true?”  
3. Avoid:
   - Wild guesses.
   - Emotional or moral judgments not in text.

**Example**

> *The museum extended its hours on weekends after noticing that many visitors were leaving before seeing all the exhibits.*

Question: What is the most reasonable inference?

A. Visitors disliked the exhibits.  
B. The museum was too crowded on weekdays.  
C. Visitors previously did not have enough time to see everything.  
D. The museum plans to raise ticket prices.

Correct: **C** (directly implied by extending hours).

**Traps:**

- Answers that are **possible** but not **supported**.
- Answers that restate the text without adding the required inference.

---

### 2.2 Category B – Craft and Structure

#### B1. Words in context

**Format:**  
Question: *“As used in line X, ‘___’ most nearly means…”* with 4 synonyms/phrases.

**Strategy:**

1. **Ignore your favorite dictionary meaning.**  
2. Reread the sentence and **one sentence before/after**.  
3. Replace each answer choice into the sentence; choose the one that keeps the meaning and tone.

**Example**

> *The scientist’s explanation was remarkably **transparent**, allowing even non‑experts to follow the complex argument.*

“As used in the text, ‘transparent’ most nearly means:

A. invisible  
B. obvious  
C. clear  
D. fragile  

- Here, “transparent” = **easy to understand** → **C**.

**Traps:**

- “Dictionary” meaning that doesn’t fit context.
- Words with similar **emotional tone** but wrong literal meaning.

---

#### B2. Text structure and purpose

**Format:**  
Questions like: *“The main purpose of the text is to…”* or *“The author includes the detail about X primarily to…”*

**Strategy:**

1. Identify **genre**: explanation, argument, narrative, description.  
2. Ask: “If I had to label this paragraph, what is it doing?” (introducing, contrasting, giving example, concluding).  
3. Match to answer that describes **function**, not content.

**Example**

> *After describing the challenges of urban noise, the author presents a study showing that planting trees can reduce sound levels by up to 5 decibels.*

Question: The author most likely includes the study to:

A. prove that all cities should plant trees.  
B. provide evidence for a possible solution to urban noise.  
C. criticize cities that ignore environmental research.  
D. argue that noise is the most serious urban problem.

Correct: **B**.

---

#### B3. Cross‑text connections

**Format:**  
Two short passages (often 1–3 sentences each). Question: *“How does Text 2 relate to Text 1?”* or *“Which choice best describes the relationship between the texts?”*

**Strategy:**

1. Summarize each text in 1 short phrase.  
2. Decide: agree, disagree, extend, provide example, provide counterexample.  
3. Choose answer that captures **both** texts accurately.

**Example**

Text 1: *“Online courses can expand access to education for students who live far from universities.”*  
Text 2: *“However, students in areas with limited internet access may find online courses difficult to use.”*

Question: How does Text 2 relate to Text 1?

A. It strongly supports the claim in Text 1.  
B. It provides a limitation to the claim in Text 1.  
C. It presents an unrelated topic.  
D. It repeats the claim in Text 1.

Correct: **B**.

---

### 2.3 Category C – Expression of Ideas

#### C1. Rhetorical synthesis

**Format:**  
You see **notes/bullets** and must choose the sentence that best combines them.

**Strategy:**

1. Identify the **shared subject** and **logical relationship** (cause/effect, contrast, sequence).  
2. Combine into **one clear, concise sentence** without redundancy.  
3. Avoid:
   - Repeating words/ideas.
   - Adding new information.

**Example**

Notes:  
- Solar panels generate electricity from sunlight.  
- They can reduce a household’s reliance on fossil fuels.  

Question: Which choice best combines the information?

A. Solar panels, which generate electricity from sunlight, can reduce a household’s reliance on fossil fuels.  
B. Solar panels generate electricity from sunlight, and fossil fuels are also used to generate electricity.  
C. Solar panels are expensive, but they generate electricity from sunlight.  
D. Solar panels generate electricity, and households use electricity.

Correct: **A** (captures both points, no extra info).

---

#### C2. Transitions

**Format:**  
Question: *“Which choice provides the most logical transition?”* or choose the best linking word (however, therefore, for example, meanwhile, etc.).

**Strategy:**

1. Read **previous sentence** and **current sentence**.  
2. Decide relationship:
   - **Contrast:** however, nevertheless, on the other hand.  
   - **Cause/effect:** therefore, as a result, consequently.  
   - **Example:** for example, for instance.  
   - **Addition:** moreover, in addition.  
3. Pick the transition that matches that relationship.

**Example**

> *Many people assume that handwriting is becoming obsolete. **____**, recent studies suggest that writing by hand can improve memory and learning.*

Best transition?

A. For example  
B. However  
C. Therefore  
D. Meanwhile  

Correct: **B** (contrast).

**Traps:**

- Transitions that “sound good” but don’t match the logic.
- Over‑general transitions like “additionally” when the sentence clearly contrasts.

### 2.4 Category D – Standard English Conventions

#### D1. Boundaries (run‑ons, fragments, comma splices)

**Core rules:**

- A **complete sentence** needs a subject + finite verb and complete thought.  
- To join two independent clauses:
  - Period: `S1. S2.`  
  - Semicolon: `S1; S2.`  
  - Comma + FANBOYS (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so).  
- **Comma splice** = two sentences joined by comma only → wrong.

**Example**

Choose the best version:

A. The experiment was a success, the results were clear.  
B. The experiment was a success; the results were clear.  
C. Because the experiment was a success. The results were clear.  
D. The experiment was a success, and the results were clear.

Correct: **B** or **D** (depending on context). A is comma splice; C is fragment.

---

#### D2. Form, structure, and sense (agreement, tense, pronouns, modifiers, parallelism)

**Key high‑frequency rules:**

1. **Subject‑verb agreement:**  
   - Ignore prepositional phrases: *“The list of items **is** long.”*  
2. **Pronoun agreement & clarity:**  
   - Singular they is allowed; pronoun must clearly refer to one noun.  
3. **Verb tense & consistency:**  
   - Use context clues (time markers, sequence).  
4. **Modifiers:**  
   - Place descriptive phrases next to what they describe.  
   - *“Walking down the street, **I** saw a dog,”* not *“Walking down the street, the dog…”*  
5. **Parallelism:**  
   - Lists and comparisons must match in form: *“to read, to write, and to think.”*

**Example (modifier)**

> *Running down the hill, the backpack slipped off Maria’s shoulders.*

Problem: It sounds like the **backpack** is running. Corrected:

> *Running down the hill, Maria felt her backpack slip off her shoulders.*

## 3. Math – complete content breakdown

Official domains: Algebra, Advanced Math, Problem‑Solving & Data Analysis, Geometry & Trig.  [College Board](https://satsuite.collegeboard.org/media/pdf/assessment-framework-for-digital-sat-suite.pdf)  [The Test Advantage](https://thetestadvantage.com/blog/digital-sat-format-2026-sections-timing-scoring-guide)  

### 3.1 Domain 1 – Algebra

#### Topic: Linear equations in one variable

**Core concept:**  
Equations of the form \(ax + b = c\). Solve by isolating \(x\).

**Formula / memory trick:**  
Think **“undo operations in reverse order”**: undo addition/subtraction, then multiplication/division.

**Example 1 (easy)**

Solve: \(3x - 5 = 16\).

- Add 5: \(3x = 21\).  
- Divide by 3: \(x = 7\).

**Example 2 (medium)**

Solve: \(\dfrac{2x - 3}{5} = 4\).

- Multiply both sides by 5: \(2x - 3 = 20\).  
- Add 3: \(2x = 23\).  
- Divide by 2: \(x = 11.5\).

**Calculator vs. no‑calculator:**  
- These are **faster by hand**; Desmos is overkill.

---

#### Topic: Linear equations in two variables & linear functions

**Core concept:**  
Equations like \(y = mx + b\), where \(m\) is slope, \(b\) is y‑intercept.

**Memory trick:**  
Slope \(m = \dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \dfrac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}\). Think **“rise over run.”**

**Example (medium)**

Line passes through \((2, 5)\) and \((6, 13)\). Find equation.

- Slope: \(m = \dfrac{13 - 5}{6 - 2} = \dfrac{8}{4} = 2\).  
- Use point‑slope: \(y - 5 = 2(x - 2)\) → \(y = 2x + 1\).

**Calculator:**  
- Desmos can plot points and fit line visually, but algebra is quick.

---

#### Topic: Systems of two linear equations

**Core concept:**  
Solve for intersection of two lines (substitution or elimination).

**Example (medium)**

\[
\begin{cases}
2x + y = 7 \\
x - y = 1
\end{cases}
\]

Add equations: \(3x = 8 \Rightarrow x = \dfrac{8}{3}\).  
Plug back: \(2(\dfrac{8}{3}) + y = 7 \Rightarrow \dfrac{16}{3} + y = 7\).  
\(y = 7 - \dfrac{16}{3} = \dfrac{21 - 16}{3} = \dfrac{5}{3}\).

**Calculator:**  
- Desmos: graph both lines, read intersection. Good check, but algebra is reliable.

---

#### Topic: Linear inequalities

**Core concept:**  
Similar to equations, but **flip inequality sign** when multiplying/dividing by a negative.

**Example**

Solve: \(-3x + 2 > 11\).

- Subtract 2: \(-3x > 9\).  
- Divide by \(-3\) → **flip sign**: \(x < -3\).

---

### 3.2 Domain 2 – Advanced Math

#### Topic: Equivalent expressions (factoring, expanding, simplifying)

**Core concept:**  
Rewrite expressions without changing value.

**Memory trick:**  
Common patterns:

- \((a + b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2\)  
- \(a^2 - b^2 = (a - b)(a + b)\)

**Example**

Factor: \(x^2 - 9\).

- Recognize \(9 = 3^2\): \(x^2 - 3^2 = (x - 3)(x + 3)\).

---

#### Topic: Quadratic equations

**Core concept:**  
Equations like \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\).

**Tools:**

- Factoring.  
- Quadratic formula:  
  \[
  x = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}
  \]
- Vertex form: \(y = a(x - h)^2 + k\).

**Example (medium)**

Solve: \(x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0\).

- Factor: \((x - 2)(x - 3) = 0\).  
- Solutions: \(x = 2, 3\).

**Calculator:**  
- Desmos: graph \(y = x^2 - 5x + 6\), read x‑intercepts.

---

#### Topic: Nonlinear systems

**Example**

\[
\begin{cases}
y = x^2 \\
y = 3x + 4
\end{cases}
\]

Set equal: \(x^2 = 3x + 4\) → \(x^2 - 3x - 4 = 0\).  
Factor: \((x - 4)(x + 1) = 0\) → \(x = 4, -1\).  
Then \(y = 16\) or \(1\).

**Desmos:**  
Graph both; intersection points appear instantly—great use of calculator.

---

#### Topic: Exponential functions

**Core concept:**  
\(y = a \cdot b^t\) (growth/decay).

- If \(b > 1\): growth.  
- If \(0 < b < 1\): decay.

**Example**

A population doubles every 5 years: \(P(t) = 1000 \cdot 2^{t/5}\).

---

### 3.3 Domain 3 – Problem‑Solving & Data Analysis

#### Topic: Ratios, rates, proportions

**Core concept:**  
\(\dfrac{a}{b} = \dfrac{c}{d}\) → cross‑multiply.

**Example**

If 3 notebooks cost \$7.50, what is the cost of 5 notebooks?

\[
\frac{3}{7.5} = \frac{5}{x} \Rightarrow 3x = 37.5 \Rightarrow x = 12.5
\]

---

#### Topic: Percentages

**Core concept:**  
“of” → multiply; “percent increase” → \(\text{new} = \text{old}(1 + r)\).

**Example**

A price increases by 20% from \$50.

- New price: \(50(1.2) = 60\).

---

#### Topic: One‑variable data (mean, median, range, SD)

- **Mean:** average.  
- **Median:** middle value.  
- **Range:** max − min.  
- **Standard deviation:** spread; larger SD = more spread out.

SAT often asks **conceptual** SD questions (which set has greater spread?) rather than computing formula.

---

#### Topic: Two‑variable data, correlation, lines of best fit

- **Positive correlation:** as x increases, y increases.  
- **Negative correlation:** as x increases, y decreases.  
- **Line of best fit:** approximate linear model.

**Example**

If scatterplot shows points trending upward, correlation is **positive**.

---

#### Topic: Probability & conditional probability

**Core concept:**  
\(\text{Probability} = \dfrac{\text{favorable}}{\text{total}}\).

Conditional: \(P(A|B) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}\).

---

#### Topic: Inference & margin of error

- Larger, random samples → better estimates.  
- Margin of error describes how far sample result may be from true population value.

SAT asks: “Is this sample representative?” or “Which conclusion is justified?”

---

### 3.4 Domain 4 – Geometry & Trigonometry

#### Reference sheet vs. memorize

You **get** formulas like: area of circle, volume of cylinder, Pythagorean theorem, etc. But you should **memorize**:

- Triangle angle sum: \(180^\circ\).  
- Special right triangles:
  - 45‑45‑90: sides \(1, 1, \sqrt{2}\).  
  - 30‑60‑90: sides \(1, \sqrt{3}, 2\).  
- Basic trig: \(\sin \theta = \dfrac{\text{opp}}{\text{hyp}}\), etc.

---

#### Lines, angles, triangles

- Vertical angles equal.  
- Alternate interior angles equal when lines are parallel.  
- Similar triangles: corresponding angles equal, sides proportional.

---

#### Right triangles & trig

**SOH‑CAH‑TOA**:

- \(\sin \theta = \dfrac{\text{Opp}}{\text{Hyp}}\)  
- \(\cos \theta = \dfrac{\text{Adj}}{\text{Hyp}}\)  
- \(\tan \theta = \dfrac{\text{Opp}}{\text{Adj}}\)

**Example**

Right triangle with hypotenuse 10, one leg 6. Find other leg.

- \(6^2 + b^2 = 10^2\) → \(36 + b^2 = 100\) → \(b^2 = 64\) → \(b = 8\).

---

#### Circles

- Circumference: \(C = 2\pi r\).  
- Area: \(A = \pi r^2\).  
- Arc length: \(\dfrac{\theta}{360^\circ} \cdot 2\pi r\).  
- Sector area: \(\dfrac{\theta}{360^\circ} \cdot \pi r^2\).

---

#### Unit circle basics on SAT

You don’t need full trig identities, but you should know:

- \(\sin 30^\circ = \dfrac{1}{2}\), \(\cos 30^\circ = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\).  
- \(\sin 45^\circ = \cos 45^\circ = \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\).  
- \(\sin 60^\circ = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\), \(\cos 60^\circ = \dfrac{1}{2}\).

## 4. Calculator strategy (Desmos on Digital SAT)

### 4.1 Where Desmos is allowed

- **All Math questions, both modules.**  
- Built‑in graphing calculator in Bluebook (powered by Desmos).  [College Board](https://satsuite.collegeboard.org/media/pdf/assessment-framework-for-digital-sat-suite.pdf)  [The Test Advantage](https://thetestadvantage.com/blog/digital-sat-format-2026-sections-timing-scoring-guide)  

### 4.2 Top 10 Desmos moves

1. **Graph equations quickly** to find intersections (solutions to systems, roots).  
2. **Check quadratic roots** by graphing and reading x‑intercepts.  
3. **Use tables** to evaluate expressions at multiple x‑values.  
4. **Plot inequalities** (e.g., `y <= 2x + 3`) to visualize solution regions.  
5. **Graph both sides** of an equation (e.g., `y = 2^x` and `y = 5x`) and find intersection.  
6. **Use sliders** (if available) to see parameter effects (helpful conceptually in practice, not on test).  
7. **Check algebra**: after solving by hand, plug solution into expression.  
8. **Visualize absolute value** and piecewise functions.  
9. **Estimate complicated intersections** when algebra is messy.  
10. **Quick sanity check**: if your answer is 1000 but graph suggests ~10, re‑check.

### 4.3 When Desmos saves 2+ minutes

- **Ugly systems** (nonlinear, messy coefficients).  
- **Complicated quadratics** with awkward factoring.  
- **Function intersection word problems** (revenue vs. cost, etc.).  
- **Graph‑based inequalities** with multiple constraints.

### 4.4 When Desmos can mislead you

- **Relying only on visual approximations** when the answer choices are close (e.g., 2.1 vs 2.2).  
- **Zoom issues:** If you don’t zoom correctly, you might miss an intersection.  
- **Over‑using it:** Simple linear equations are faster by hand.

Rule of thumb:  
If you can solve in **≤30 seconds by hand**, do it. If algebra looks messy or involves multiple equations, **consider Desmos**.

## 5. Scoring strategy & adaptive test psychology

### 5.1 Why Module 1 is critical

- Module 1 performance determines whether you see the **hard** or **easier** Module 2.  
- To reach 1500+, you almost certainly need **hard modules in both sections**.  
- That means:
  - **High accuracy** in Module 1.  
  - **Controlled pacing**—don’t rush and bleed points.

### 5.2 Optimal time per question

**Reading & Writing:** 54 questions / 64 minutes ≈ **71 seconds per question**.

- Think: **1:10 per question**.  
- Micro‑strategy:
  - If stuck at 1:15–1:20, **eliminate, guess, flag, move on**.

**Math:** 44 questions / 70 minutes ≈ **95 seconds per question**.

- Think: **1.5 minutes per question** on average.  
- Some will take 30–40 seconds; a few may take 2–3 minutes.

### 5.3 Guessing policy

- **No penalty for wrong answers.** Always answer everything.  [test-ninjas.com](https://test-ninjas.com/digital-sat-format)  
- If time is nearly up:
  - Rapidly fill in guesses for remaining questions (don’t leave blanks).
  - Use any partial elimination you’ve done.

### 5.4 Handling the “hard module”

If Module 2 feels hard, that’s **good news**—you likely did well in Module 1.

Mindset:

- “Hard” doesn’t mean you’re failing; it means you’re in the **high‑score lane**.  
- Expect to **feel unsure** on several questions; that’s normal at 700–800 level.  
- Focus on:
  - **Securing the ones you know.**  
  - **Smart guessing** on the truly brutal ones.

### 5.5 Process of elimination (POE)

RW:

- Eliminate answers that:
  - Contradict the passage.  
  - Add new information.  
  - Are too extreme.  
- Often you’re left with 2; then ask: “Which one is **exactly** supported?”

Math:

- Plug in answer choices (backsolving).  
- Check units and reasonableness.  
- Eliminate negative/too large/too small values quickly.

## 8. Section‑specific rapid improvement tips

### 8.1 Reading & Writing

**How to gain 50–100 points in 3 weeks**

- **Narrow focus:**  
  - Fix **sentence boundaries + agreement** (fastest grammar gains).  
  - Drill **main idea + inference** daily.  
- **Daily routine (45–60 min):**
  - 10 grammar questions.  
  - 10 reading questions (main idea/inference/evidence).  
  - Review every wrong answer thoroughly.

**#1 RW mistake:**  
Reading **too slowly** and treating passages like English class instead of a **targeted skill test**.

Fix:

- Read only what you need.  
- For each question, ask: “What exact line/phrase proves this?”  
- If you can’t point to it, you’re guessing.

**Boring/confusing passage approach:**

1. Accept that you don’t need to “like” it.  
2. Read **first sentence carefully** (often main idea).  
3. Skim rest for structure: examples, contrast, conclusion.  
4. Answer questions by **hunting for evidence**, not by “feel.”

**Top 8 grammar rules that show up constantly**

1. **Subject‑verb agreement** (including tricky singular/plural subjects).  
2. **Pronoun agreement & clarity**.  
3. **Verb tense & sequence** (especially present vs. past).  
4. **Comma vs. semicolon vs. period** (run‑ons, comma splices).  
5. **Introductory comma** after long opening phrase.  
6. **Nonessential clauses** (comma pairs around extra info).  
7. **Parallel structure** in lists and comparisons.  
8. **Modifier placement** (avoid dangling modifiers).

---

### 8.2 Math

**How to gain 50–100 points in 3 weeks**

- Identify **2–3 core domains** where you miss most questions (often algebra, quadratics, ratios).  
- Spend **70% of math time** on those domains.  
- Use **UWorld or similar QBank** for high‑volume, high‑quality practice if available.  [katalystprep.com](https://katalystprep.com/reviews/sat/uworld/)  [Charlotte Observer](https://www.charlotteobserver.com/careers-education/uworld-sat-prep-review/)  

**Plugging in numbers & backsolving**

- **Plug in numbers** when variables represent categories (e.g., “x adults and y children”).  
- **Backsolve** when answer choices are numbers:
  - Start with middle choice.  
  - Plug into equation or condition.  
  - Narrow up/down.

**Avoiding careless errors**

- Underline **what the question is actually asking** (e.g., “value of 2x,” not x).  
- Box units (meters vs. centimeters).  
- When you finish a question quickly, **spend 5 seconds checking**:
  - Did I answer the right thing?  
  - Does the answer make sense (sign, magnitude)?

**Word problem translation**

1. Identify **unknowns** and assign variables.  
2. Translate phrases:
   - “more than” → `+`.  
   - “less than” → subtract in correct order.  
   - “per” → division or rate.  
3. Write equation(s) before doing any arithmetic.

## 10. Exam day complete playbook

### 10.1 Night before

**Review:**

- Your **personal error log** (top patterns).  
- Grammar rules list.  
- Key math formulas & special triangles.  

**Do NOT:**

- Take a full test.  
- Learn brand‑new topics.

**Sleep & logistics:**

- Aim for **7–9 hours** of sleep.  
- Pack:
  - Approved device with Bluebook installed & charged.  
  - Charger, ID, admission info, snacks, water.  
- Plan route and arrival time (aim to be **30–40 minutes early**).

### 10.2 Test morning

- Light breakfast with **protein + complex carbs** (e.g., eggs + toast, yogurt + granola).  
- Brief 10–15 minute warm‑up:
  - 3–5 RW questions.  
  - 3–5 math questions.

At the center:

- Check in, set up device, follow proctor instructions.  
- Confirm Bluebook test is loaded.

### 10.3 During the exam

**Module 1 strategy (both sections):**

- Aim for **high accuracy**; this is your ticket to the hard module.  
- If stuck:
  - 30–40 seconds of genuine effort.  
  - Eliminate, guess, flag, move on.

**Module 2 strategy:**

- If it feels hard: **good sign**—you’re in high‑score territory.  
- Don’t panic if you can’t fully solve a few questions; focus on maximizing correct answers overall.

**With 5 minutes left in a module:**

1. Make sure **every question has an answer** (guess if needed).  
2. Revisit **flagged questions** in order of easiest to hardest.  
3. Don’t change answers unless you see a **clear mistake**.

**Staying calm under pressure:**

- Use a simple breathing pattern:
  - Inhale for 4 counts, hold for 4, exhale for 6.  
  - Do this 2–3 times between questions if you feel anxious.
- Remind yourself: **You’ve practiced this exact format.**

### 10.4 After the exam

- Scores usually release within about a week.  [The Test Advantage](https://thetestadvantage.com/blog/digital-sat-format-2026-sections-timing-scoring-guide)  
- When scores arrive:
  - Compare to your target.  
  - If you’re within ~50–70 points of your goal and still have time, a **focused 4–6 week retake plan** can realistically push you over.

Ask yourself:

- Did I lose more points to **content gaps** or **careless mistakes**?  
- Did pacing feel rushed or comfortable?  

Use that to decide whether to retake and how to adjust your prep.
